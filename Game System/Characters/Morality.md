Your morality is defined by five pairs of opposing scores.

| Self       | Others     |
| ---------- | ---------- |
| Control    | Impulse    |
| Aggression | Harmony    |
| Trust      | Deceit     |
| Growth     | Stagnation |

Each morality score has a maximum level of 85, and each pair has a total of 100 at all times. When one of your scores goes up, the other goes down so the pair's total remains at 100.

## Morality Checks

You may need to roll your morals in two different situations.

- As a part the [[Gameplay#Effective Score|effective score]] calculated for all checks.
- As a standalone check ([[Chances Roll|chances roll]]) to determine how your character should react in a certain situation. You roll a d% and depending on where it lands, you follow that side of the morality axis.

> You are brought inside the kingdom's treasury when an alarm sounds. The guard accompanying you runs to check what is happening, forgetting all about you. You are left alone with a lot of wealth within grasp. As a player, you know that it's a bad idea to steal, but your character is impulsive. Your GM asks you to make a control/impulse check, in which you have a score of 38/62. You roll a d% against 62 and get a 52, which means that you should act impulsively. You decide that stealing a small number of very valuable gems should satisfy your character, which you do, disregarding the possible consequences you as a player are afraid of.

## Morality Changes

You may roll an [[Resources#Expenditure/Accumulation|accumulation]] (but not expenditure) check on a moral when:

- The GM asks for it after a scene, or you consider that you need to make an accumulation check. Determined on what transpired, the action may had lesser, normal, or greater impact:
	- lesser impact: max increase to 30
	- normal impact: max increase to 60
	- major impact: max increase to 85
- As a [[Timekeeping#Downtime|downtime activity]] to change your morality.
